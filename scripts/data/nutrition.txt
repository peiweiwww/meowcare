Cats are obligate carnivores, which means they rely on nutrients that come primarily from animal-based proteins. A balanced cat diet should include adequate protein, essential fatty acids, vitamins, and minerals formulated specifically for feline needs. Foods made for dogs or homemade meals without veterinary guidance may leave important nutritional gaps.

When choosing a cat food, look for options labeled complete and balanced for the cat's life stage. Kittens, adults, and seniors have different energy and nutrient requirements. Portion size matters too. Overfeeding can contribute to obesity, while underfeeding can lead to weight loss and poor muscle condition. Measuring meals and monitoring body condition over time can help you keep feeding on track.

Treats should be limited so they do not crowd out complete nutrition. Fresh water should always be available alongside food. If your cat has a medical condition such as kidney disease, food allergies, or diabetes, work with a veterinarian to choose the safest diet. Good nutrition supports energy, coat quality, immune function, and overall well-being.
