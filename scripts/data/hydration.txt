Cats often have a naturally low thirst drive, which means they may not drink enough water on their own. Keeping a cat hydrated starts with making water easy to access and appealing. Place several clean water bowls around the home, especially in quiet places away from litter boxes. Many cats prefer wide bowls that do not press on their whiskers, while others are more interested in running water from a fountain.

Wet food can also play a major role in hydration because it contains much more moisture than dry kibble. If your cat eats mostly dry food, ask your veterinarian whether adding canned food would be a good idea. You can also mix a small amount of warm water into wet meals to increase moisture intake even more. Any diet changes should be gradual so your cat adjusts comfortably.

Pay attention to signs of dehydration such as dry gums, low energy, sunken eyes, or reduced skin elasticity. Dehydration can happen more quickly in hot weather or during illness. If your cat is vomiting, has diarrhea, refuses to drink, or seems weak, contact a veterinarian promptly. Consistent access to fresh water and moisture-rich meals are some of the simplest ways to support long-term feline health.
