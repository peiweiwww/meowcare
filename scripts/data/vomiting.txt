Occasional vomiting in cats can happen for simple reasons such as eating too quickly, swallowing hair, or reacting to a sudden diet change. Even so, vomiting should never be ignored if it becomes frequent or is paired with other symptoms. A single mild episode in an otherwise normal cat may be monitored closely, but repeated vomiting deserves veterinary attention.

If your cat vomits, remove food for a short period only if your veterinarian has advised that approach, and make sure fresh water remains available unless your cat cannot keep water down. Watch for warning signs such as lethargy, diarrhea, abdominal pain, blood in vomit, trouble breathing, or refusal to eat. These can point to a more serious issue including dehydration, toxin exposure, intestinal blockage, or underlying disease.

Keep notes about when the vomiting started, what the vomit looked like, and whether your cat recently ate something unusual. That information can help your veterinarian assess the problem. Seek urgent care right away if your cat is vomiting repeatedly, seems weak, has a swollen abdomen, or may have swallowed a string, medication, or toxic substance.
